## Introduction

State governments sit at an underappreciated intersection of political philosophy and practical economics. Unlike the federal government, states must balance budgets, compete for residents and businesses, and make real tradeoffs with finite resources. This post begins a series examining how states raise money, spend it, and what that reveals about governance priorities.

## Key Points

- The basics of state revenue: income taxes, sales taxes, property taxes, and fees
- How interstate tax competition shapes policy decisions
- The tension between fiscal responsibility and service provision
- What bond ratings and pension liabilities reveal about long-term governance
- Case studies in states that have gotten it right and wrong

## Conclusion

State finances are a window into what a government actually values, as opposed to what it says it values. The constraints are real and the tradeoffs are visible. Understanding them is essential to thinking clearly about governance.
